Abstract

Akashi Kaikyō Bridge

Resonance plays a critical role in the performance, safety, and longevity of modern infrastructure. When a structure’s natural frequency aligns with external forces (such as wind, seismic activity, or human motion), the resulting adjustment could result in catastrophic consequences. They can be prevented through new precautionary measures made before construction. This article examines the evolution of resonance awareness and mitigation strategies in civil engineering through notable historical examples. Early disasters, including the calamitous 1995 Kobe Earthquakein Japan (Aldrich et al, 2011), ultimately lead to the adoption of aerodynamic deck designs, tuned mass dampers, and seismic isolation systems. Japan’s post-Kobe reconstruction ushered in a global shift toward performance-based design, where resonance is analysed and controlled. This includes successes such as the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, Tokyo Skytree, and Taipei 101, demonstrating how the adoption of new protective measures has ensured our security. Through comparative analysis of failed and successful designs, this study highlights how monitoring resonance has become an essential design parameter for resilient, adaptive infrastructure in earthquake and wind-prone regions worldwide.

Introduction

Modern resonance-control techniques in infrastructure have largely developed in response to past structural failures. Disasters such as the 1995 Kobe Earthquake demonstrated how buildings and bridges could suffer severe damage when their natural frequencies aligned with near-fault ground motions. This event accelerated the adoption of base isolation, energy-dissipating devices, and advanced tuned mass damping in Japan and worldwide (Siringoringo et al, 2008).

To achieve the successful construction of any high-rise building in cities or elsewhere, it will require the attention and meticulous efforts of the many fields of civil engineering, in which they procure from their past achievements, to ensure secure architectural design, site conditions, the choice of building materials, construction design, development of building technology and its safe and durable operation (Teplyshev et al, 2016).

As a result, many modern structures—including Taipei 101, the Tokyo Skytree, and the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge—are equipped with large-scale TMDs, seismic isolators, and real-time structural health monitoring (SHM) systems that continually track changes in vibration. These technologies directly reflect lessons learned from past failures, marking a shift from passive resistance to actively controlling resonance to ensure greater safety and resilience (Song et al, 2014).

Passive Damping Systems

Similarly to a guitar, skyscrapers can also function as an oscillator; wind or any other seismic activity, like earthquakes, may cause excitation to the building, causing it to sway. Although it may be relatively harmless to the infrastructure itself, the sudden motion may cause unnecessary distress to the occupants inside (Practical Engineering, 2016; Teplyshev et al, 2016), with Table 1 providing people's susceptibility to the changes in acceleration.

To reduce unwanted vibrations in buildings, bridges and towers, engineers have begun utilising ‘Tuned Mass Dampers’ (TMD). Consisting of a secondary mass connected to the main structure through springs and damping elements, the key idea is that the TMD is “tuned” to match the natural frequency of the structure (BSBG, 2017). When the building or bridge begins to vibrate, the damper mass moves out of phase with the structure, creating a counteracting force that reduces the overall motion.

Springs are crucial for tuning the frequency and provide the necessary stiffness to ensure optimal performance. Damping elements, either hydraulic or frictional, play a key role in converting kinetic energy into heat, thereby dissipating energy. For maximum energy dissipation, they are designed for a range of eigenfrequencies, for when precise frequencies aren’t known, making TMDs versatile and effective solutions for vibration control (Miyamoto International, 2025).

Overall, the applications of TMDs are diverse, ranging from skyscrapers to pedestrian bridges, becoming more widely adopted during the late 20th century as buildings grew taller and more flexible, and they can be retrofitted into existing structures; the purpose is not only to increase safety but also to improve serviceability and extend the lifespan of the structure (Teplyshev et al, 2016).

In addition to TMDs, researchers are studying new types of cost-efficient dampers for mitigating wind and earthquake induced vibrations in tall buildings and other structures. A Tuned Liquid Damper (TLD) is a type of TMD where the mass is replaced by a liquid (Practical Engineering, 2025). Tuned Liquid Column Dampers (TLCDs) are a special type of TLDs relying on the motion of a column of liquid in a U-tube like container to counteract the forces acting on the structure. However, unlike TMDs, is amplitude dependent, and thus the TLCD dynamics are non-linear (Table 2).

The advantages of TLCD systems include low cost and maintenance and containers can be utilized for building water supply, unlike a TMD where the dead weight of the mass has no other functional use. Some of the innovative applications for liquid dampers studied in the past were in ship stabilization, satellite stabilization and recently in building applications.

Operational Modal Analysis

Operational Modal Analysis is a form of Structural Health Monitoring (SHM), in which it is one of the techniques used to investigate the dynamics of a structure - determining mode shapes, damping, natural frequencies from operational vibration measurements. Other techniques are typically performed in controlled environments, whereas OMA is performed in-situation, under real-world load conditions (SIEMENS, 2020).

Seismic Isolation

Seismic isolation is a structural protection technique used to reduce the forces that buildings and bridges experience during an earthquake. Instead of relying only on strength to resist shaking, seismic isolation works by separating the structure from the ground. This is done by placing special bearings or sliding devices between the foundation and the rest of the structure, allowing the building or bridge to move more slowly and independently of the violent ground motion beneath it. By increasing the natural period of the structure and limiting the amount of acceleration that reaches it, isolation helps to reduce damage and improve safety.

Common isolation systems include lead rubber bearings, high damping rubber bearings and friction pendulum systems. Lead rubber bearings were developed by Bill Robinson in New Zealand in 1975, while James M Kelly carried out major theoretical work in the field. These systems offer both flexibility and energy dissipation, and they absorb much of the earthquake energy before it reaches the building or bridge.

Seismic isolation became more widely used in Japan after the 1995 Hanshin (Kobe) earthquake highlighted the weaknesses of traditional fixed base structures. Today, many isolated buildings, hospitals and bridge overpasses show excellent performance in strong earthquakes, making seismic isolation one of the most reliable methods for protecting infrastructure in active seismic regions.

Successes & Failures

Resonance control has become an essential part of modern structural engineering, and its effectiveness is clear when comparing successful applications with well known failures. After the 1995 Hanshin (Kobe) earthquake, engineers in Japan recognised that many structures resonated with the ground motion, leading to serious damage. In response, Japan expanded the use of seismic isolation, tuned mass dampers and energy dissipating devices. These methods have been highly successful in later earthquakes. Base isolated buildings and hospitals in Kobe and other regions have shown significantly reduced damage, and the Akashi Kaikyo Bridge has performed exceptionally well thanks to its tuned mass dampers and aerodynamic shaping. Even though the 1995 earthquake shifted its towers during construction, the completed bridge has demonstrated excellent stability because its resonance behaviour was carefully controlled from the beginning.

A similar success is seen in Taipei 101, which contains a 660 tonne tuned mass damper suspended near its upper floors. This device moves out of phase with the building during strong winds or ground shaking, reducing sway and protecting the structure. Recordings during typhoons and earthquakes show the damper operating exactly as intended, proving the value of resonance based design.

In contrast, the failure of the London Millennium Bridge in 2000 shows what happens when resonance is not properly anticipated. The bridge experienced synchronous lateral excitation, a form of pedestrian induced resonance, which caused it to sway dramatically only hours after opening. Unlike the Japanese examples, the bridge did not include adequate damping systems. It was later stabilised by installing tuned mass dampers and viscous dampers beneath the deck. The comparison demonstrates that resonance must be understood and managed: when addressed, it leads to safe and resilient structures, but when ignored, it can result in rapid and unexpected failure.
