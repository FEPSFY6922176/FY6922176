Abstract

Resonance plays a critical role in the performance, safety, and longevity of modern infrastructure. When a structure’s natural frequency aligns with external dynamic forces (such as wind, seismic activity, or human motion), which the resulting amplification can lead to catastrophic failure or, when properly controlled, enhanced resilience. This article examines the evolution of resonance awareness and mitigation strategies in civil engineering through notable historical and contemporary examples. Early disasters, including the 1995 Kobe Earthquake[6] in Japan, which had forsakened Japan in devastation, ultimately lead to the adoption of aerodynamic deck designs, tuned mass dampers, and seismic isolation systems; Japan’s post-Kobe reconstruction ushered in a global shift toward performance-based design, where resonance is analyzed and controlled. This includes successes such as the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, Tokyo Skytree, and Taipei 101, demonstrating how tuned and base-isolated systems now safeguard against wind and seismic excitation. Through comparative analysis of failed and successful designs, this study highlights how monitoring resonance has become an essential design parameter for resilient, adaptive infrastructure in earthquake and wind-prone regions worldwide.

Introduction

Modern resonance-control techniques in infrastructure have largely developed in response to past structural failures. Early disasters such as the 1940 Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse revealed how wind-induced resonance and aeroelastic flutter could cause catastrophic oscillations, leading engineers to introduce aerodynamic deck shaping and the first forms of damping devices. Later, the 1995 Kobe Earthquake demonstrated how buildings and bridges could suffer severe damage when their natural frequencies aligned with near-fault ground motions. This event accelerated the adoption of base isolation, energy-dissipating devices, and advanced tuned mass damping in Japan and worldwide (Fujino and Siringoringo 2013).

As a result, many modern structures—including Taipei 101, the Tokyo Skytree, and the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge—are equipped with large-scale TMDs, seismic isolators, and real-time structural health monitoring (SHM) systems that continually track changes in vibration. These technologies directly reflect lessons learned from past failures, marking a shift from passive resistance to actively controlling resonance to ensure greater safety and resilience.

Kobe

The Great Hanshin Earthquake struck Kobe on 17 January 1995 with a magnitude of 6.9, causing over 6,400 deaths and massive destruction to buildings, highways, and rail systems. Severe ground shaking and soil failure led to collapsed expressways and widespread infrastructure damage. The disaster prompted major reforms in Japan’s seismic design, including improved damping, base isolation, and structural monitoring technologies.
