Abstract

Resonance plays a critical role in the performance, safety, and longevity of modern infrastructure. When a structure’s natural frequency aligns with external dynamic 
forces (such as wind, seismic activity, or human motion), which the resulting amplification can lead to catastrophic failure or, when properly controlled, enhanced resilience. 
This article examines the evolution of resonance awareness and mitigation strategies in civil engineering through notable historical and contemporary examples. 
Early disasters, including the 1995 Great Hanshin-Awaji (Kobe) Earthquake in Japan, revealed the destructive potential of resonance, leading to the adoption of aerodynamic 
deck designs, tuned mass dampers, and seismic isolation systems. Japan’s post-Kobe reconstruction ushered in a global shift toward performance-based design, where resonance 
is analyzed and controlled rather than avoided. Successes such as the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, Tokyo Skytree, and Taipei 101 demonstrate how tuned and base-isolated systems 
now safeguard against wind and seismic excitation. Through comparative analysis of failed and successful designs, this study highlights how resonance—once a cause of 
collapse—has become an essential design parameter for resilient, adaptive infrastructure in earthquake- and wind-prone regions worldwide.
