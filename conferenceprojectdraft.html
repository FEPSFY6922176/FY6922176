Abstract

Resonance plays a critical role in the performance, safety, and longevity of modern infrastructure. When a structure’s natural frequency aligns with external dynamic forces (such as wind, seismic activity, or human motion), which the resulting amplification can lead to catastrophic failure or, when properly controlled, enhanced resilience. This article examines the evolution of resonance awareness and mitigation strategies in civil engineering through notable historical and contemporary examples. Early disasters, including the 1995 Kobe Earthquake[6] in Japan, which had forsakened Japan in devastation, ultimately lead to the adoption of aerodynamic deck designs, tuned mass dampers, and seismic isolation systems; Japan’s post-Kobe reconstruction ushered in a global shift toward performance-based design, where resonance is analyzed and controlled. This includes successes such as the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, Tokyo Skytree, and Taipei 101, demonstrating how tuned and base-isolated systems now safeguard against wind and seismic excitation. Through comparative analysis of failed and successful designs, this study highlights how monitoring resonance has become an essential design parameter for resilient, adaptive infrastructure in earthquake and wind-prone regions worldwide.

Introduction

Resonance occurs when a structure is exposed to repeated vibrations at a frequency that matches its own natural frequency (the rate at which it naturally tends to move back and forth). When this happens, even small vibrations can grow much stronger, leading to dangerous oscillations. Every structure has one or more natural frequencies that depend on its shape, materials, and stiffness. For an example, a tall, flexible building will have a much lower natural frequency compared to a short, stiff structure. If the external forces — such as wind gusts, earthquakes, or traffic — occur at or near these frequencies, resonance can amplify the motion, sometimes enough to cause cracking, or even collapse.

Because of this, engineers spend a great deal of time measuring and monitoring vibration behaviour during and after construction. The most common method is called operational modal analysis (OMA)[1], where sensors are placed on a bridge or building to record its motion under normal conditions such as traffic, wind, or minor tremors. These recordings are then analysed to identify the structure’s natural frequencies, mode shapes (how it bends or twists), and damping ratios (how quickly the motion dies out). Modern bridges often include permanent vibration sensors or accelerometers built into the structure, allowing engineers to monitor resonance in real time. This kind of system, known as structural health monitoring (SHM)[2], helps detect changes over time; for instance, if a bridge becomes slightly weaker or its frequency shifts due to damage or aging.

During construction, engineers also perform forced-vibration tests or ambient vibration tests to ensure that the measured natural frequencies match design predictions. International standards such as ISO 4866[1] describe how to measure and interpret these vibrations safely. From further studies, a new method was introduced: the frequency-domain decomposition (FDD)[3] method, which is widely used to extract modal data from field measurements. Research by Fujino and Siringoringo (2013)[4] on bridge monitoring in Japan demonstrates how continuous vibration measurements can track changes in bridge stiffness and detect potential resonance problems before they become dangerous. In practice, this means engineers can “tune” a structure during or after construction — for example, by adding dampers or adjusting stiffness — to prevent resonance with expected environmental forces.

In recent decades, many of the world’s most advanced structures have been designed with built-in systems to control resonance. Towering buildings such as the Taipei 101 Tower in Taiwan and the Shanghai Tower in China use massive tuned mass dampers (TMDs)[5] weighing hundreds of tonnes to reduce swaying during typhoons and earthquakes. These devices move out of phase with the building’s motion, absorbing energy and reducing acceleration. Japan had the most success in developing and applying the new technology, incorporating both active and passive damping systems in skyscrapers like the Yokohama Landmark Tower and Tokyo Skytree. The Skytree’s central reinforced-concrete core acts as a giant counter-mass, effectively functioning as an internal damper that improves seismic and wind performance[4]. Similarly, modern bridges such as the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge employ aerodynamic shaping and tuned damping devices within their towers and cables to control oscillations during strong winds or earthquakes. These innovations illustrate how learning from the Kobe Earthquake[6] have transformed resonance from an unprecedented destructive phenomenon into a more predictable and controlled design factor, significantly improving structural safety, durability, and public confidence.
