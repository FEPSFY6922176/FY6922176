Abstract

Akashi Kaikyō Bridge

Resonance plays a critical role in the performance, safety, and longevity of modern infrastructure. When a structure’s natural frequency aligns with external forces (such as wind, seismic activity, or human motion), the resulting adjustment could result in catastrophic consequences, which can be prevented through new precautionary measures made before construction. This article examines the evolution of resonance awareness and mitigation strategies in civil engineering through notable historical examples. Early disasters, including the calamitous 1995 Kobe Earthquake[6] in Japan, ultimately lead to the adoption of aerodynamic deck designs, tuned mass dampers, and seismic isolation systems; Japan’s post-Kobe reconstruction ushered in a global shift toward performance-based design, where resonance is analyzed and controlled. This includes successes such as the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge, Tokyo Skytree, and Taipei 101, demonstrating how the adoption of new protective measures has ensured our security. Through comparative analysis of failed and successful designs, this study highlights how monitoring resonance has become an essential design parameter for resilient, adaptive infrastructure in earthquake and wind-prone regions worldwide.

Introduction

Modern resonance-control techniques in infrastructure have largely developed in response to past structural failures. Early disasters such as the 1940 Tacoma Narrows Bridge collapse revealed how wind-induced resonance and aeroelastic flutter could cause catastrophic oscillations, leading engineers to introduce aerodynamic deck shaping and the first forms of damping devices. Later, the 1995 Kobe Earthquake demonstrated how buildings and bridges could suffer severe damage when their natural frequencies aligned with near-fault ground motions. This event accelerated the adoption of base isolation, energy-dissipating devices, and advanced tuned mass damping in Japan and worldwide (Fujino and Siringoringo 2013).

As a result, many modern structures—including Taipei 101, the Tokyo Skytree, and the Akashi Kaikyō Bridge—are equipped with large-scale TMDs, seismic isolators, and real-time structural health monitoring (SHM) systems that continually track changes in vibration. These technologies directly reflect lessons learned from past failures, marking a shift from passive resistance to actively controlling resonance to ensure greater safety and resilience.

Kobe 1995

The Great Hanshin Earthquake struck Kobe on 17 January 1995 with a magnitude of 6.9, causing over 6,400 deaths and massive destruction to buildings, highways, and rail systems. Severe ground shaking and soil failure led to collapsed expressways and widespread infrastructure damage. Entire neighbourhoods were destroyed, and many critical lifelines failed.

Tuned Mass Dampers

Tuned Mass Dampers (TMDs) are devices used in buildings and bridges to control excessive vibrations by counteracting motion at specific frequencies. A TMD typically consists of a large mass attached to the structure through springs and dampers. It is “tuned” so that its natural frequency matches the structure’s dominant vibration mode.

When the structure begins to sway due to wind, earthquakes, or traffic, the TMD moves out of phase, producing an equal and opposite force that reduces overall vibration. This motion dissipates energy as heat and improves comfort and safety.

TMDs became widely adopted during the late 20th century as buildings grew taller and more flexible. Modern TMDs may be passive, semi-active, or active, and are increasingly paired with digital monitoring systems to maintain tuning accuracy as structural properties evolve over time.
